## Question 1
Question Type: Direct Concept
Difficulty Level: Foundation
Question Text: What does a bar graph mainly help us do?
Options:
- A. Measure angles
- B. Compare quantities easily
- C. Find area of shapes
- D. Solve equations
Correct Answer: B
Explanation: A bar graph shows data using bars of equal width, making comparison between categories easy. The other options belong to geometry or algebra, not data handling.
Common Mistake: Thinking every graph is used for measurement rather than comparison.
Learning Objective: Recognize the purpose of a bar graph.

Question Text: Which of the following can be found directly from a frequency table? Select all that apply.
Options:
- A. The category with the highest frequency
- B. The total number of observations
- C. The exact color of the bars in a future graph
- D. Whether two categories have equal frequency
Correct Answer: A, B, D
Explanation: A frequency table directly shows category counts, so we can identify the highest category, total observations, and equal frequencies. The color of future graph bars is a design choice, not information from the table.
Common Mistake: Believing presentation details are part of the data itself.
Learning Objective: Distinguish between data facts and display choices.

## Question 50
Question Type: Short Answer
Difficulty Level: Advanced
Question Text: The numbers of medals won by five houses are 14, 11, 9, 15, and 16. If the house with 9 medals wins 4 more medals, will it move ahead of exactly two houses? Explain with the new order.
Correct Answer: `No. It will have 13 medals, so the new order is 16, 15, 14, 13, 11. It moves ahead of only one house.`
Explanation: The house with 9 medals becomes 13 after winning 4 more. Comparing with the others gives 16, 15, 14, 13, and 11. So it moves above only the house with 11 medals, not exactly two houses.
Common Mistake: Comparing only with the immediate next house and not reordering the full list.
Learning Objective: Reorganize and interpret updated data after a change.
